### Introduction: The Mask of Unawareness

Political systems that present themselves outwardly as moral, democratic, or peace-oriented often employ subtly orchestrated control mechanisms internally, relying on collective resonance rather than individual decision-making. The conscious production of perpetrators is not a side effect but a central steering instrument: it shifts responsibility from the individual to the field and thus creates a resonance space in which guilt is collectively redistributed and control is seemingly legitimized.

Under the guise of ignorance or powerlessness, social groups, movements, or individuals are deliberately marked, excluded, and pushed into a perpetrator role through media and institutional resonance processes. This dynamic is circular and self-reinforcing: What is officially fought or morally condemned is systematically reproduced and amplified—not because individuals plan it, but because the resonance field itself perpetuates the underlying impulses.

**Central Dynamic:**  
What appears on the surface as a determined fight against problems is strategically amplified within the resonance field and instrumentalized for subsequent legitimization of control. Thus, perpetrator production is not an individual but a systemic-collective phenomenon that escapes responsibility by distributing guilt across the entire field, thereby creating new spaces for control, surveillance, and intervention.

### 1. Step-by-Step Mechanism of Perpetrator Production

**a) Marking:**  
A group or person is publicly labeled as a "problem" or "threat." The systemic center—comprising political parties, elites, mainstream media, and expert circles—sets targeted impulses and channels societal attention to the marked object.  
→ Marking is never purely individual but is taken up and amplified by the collective resonance field.

**b) Exclusion:**  
Social, political, or media mechanisms take effect: isolation, ridicule, defamation, administrative exclusion, algorithmic invisibilization.  
→ The audience, public discourse, and subsystems take over the exclusion, while in the background, hidden steering circles (lobbying, networks, think tanks, international interests) adjust the direction.

**c) Resonance Amplification:**  
Exclusion creates reactance: resistance, defiance, anger, radicalization. The group marked as "perpetrator" grows through the collective reaction—the system creates the perpetrator it needs.  
→ Outsiders and groups become projection screens for collective emotions, fears, and attribution of guilt.

**d) Media Multiplication:**  
Every reaction of the excluded is picked up, staged in the media, scandalized, and interpreted as a danger or as proof of the original marking.  
→ Media, social networks, influencers, and alternative platforms reinforce, mirror, and polarize the dynamic—feedback and escalation are accelerated.

**e) Escalation:**  
The interplay between exclusion and media resonance drives radicalization: spirals of outrage, mobilization, counteractions, antagonism.  
→ Systemic support—openly through resources, covertly through sabotage, information steering, targeted provocations—accelerates and intensifies the climate of escalation.

**f) Systemic Support (covert/open):**  
Networks, lobbying groups, informal circles, and international actors deliberately provide resources: funding, logistics, legal and communicative support, data, safe spaces, or targeted information sharing.  
→ Support can be open (e.g., public funding) or subtle (e.g., targeted leaks, narratives, data streams).

**g) Reversal of Guilt:**  
At the moment of highest escalation, responsibility is not assigned to individual actors but to the entire field—audience, voters, society:  
*"You elected them. You let it happen. We had to intervene."*  
→ Guilt is collectivized; the resonance field bears responsibility—the system evades individual attribution.

**h) Re-Legitimation of Control:**  
The danger generated through escalation is used to legitimize new control measures: laws, surveillance, restrictions of basic rights, administrative interventions, new control instruments.  
→ The control structures are strengthened and stabilized, while the original dynamic continues to operate within the resonance field—only the roles and justifications rotate.

### 2. Role Explication – Who Is Involved?

The strategy of controlled derailment only unfolds its effect through the interplay of multiple systemic roles, which are explicitly and implicitly embedded in the resonance field:

- **Systemic Center:** Political parties, elites, mainstream media, economic actors, supranational organizations, and international players. They provide impulses, set interpretive frameworks, and initiate central feedback loops. Their influence is not only direct but operates through framing, agenda-setting, and selective attention.
- **Marked Outsiders:** Individuals, groups, movements, opposition structures, minorities, or "deviants." They are systematically shifted and forced into the perpetrator role—regardless of their actual position or intention. Their marking is part of the overall architecture.
- **Media Amplifiers:** Press, social networks, influencers, alternative media platforms, and opinion leaders. They multiply, mirror, and polarize the dynamic—resonance is amplified, fragmented, and circulated among the actors.
- **Covert Steering Circles:** Lobby groups, financial networks, think tanks, intelligence structures, international interest groups. They act behind the scenes, provide targeted resources, direct information flows, orchestrate narratives, and covertly influence the system dynamics.
- **Audience/Mass:** Electorate, spectators, observers, digital followers, "silent majority"—they mirror, amplify, and legitimize collective dynamics through their resonance, passivity, or active participation. Their role is fluid: oscillating between amplification, legitimization, obstruction, and latent irritation.

> *Each of these roles—whether visible or hidden, active or passive—is an integral part of the resonance field. Impact, responsibility, and guilt are systemically shared and only arise through the interplay of all elements.*

**3.3 Role Rotation:**  
Victim and perpetrator roles are permeable within the resonance field. The system structurally blanks out its own co-responsibility. Guilt is externalized, dynamics are presented as "natural" or "without alternative." This creates the illusion of individual guilt and the possibility of systemic self-exculpation.

**3.6 Resonance Rule: Collective Co-Responsibility:**  
Within the complete resonance field, every member—regardless of visibility, naming, or explicit statement—bears systemic co-responsibility. Impact and guilt arise circularly within the field, not within the individual.

### 4. Historical Example: Hitler as Product of Systemic Dynamics

The example of Hitler demonstrates the systemic logic of perpetrator production in its most extreme form: After World War I, Hitler initially moved within the circles of socialist workers’ movements, participated as an observer in SPD-affiliated circles, and experienced early exclusion as well as political and social marginalization. The subsequent transformation—his rise from the DAP to the NSDAP—was not enabled by personal ambition alone, but by a complex interplay of resonance fields, networks, resource influx, and international interests.

Hitler became a projection surface for collective fears, discontent, and the assignment of blame. Systemic dynamics—including targeted exclusion, media amplification, covert support, resource allocation, and international observation—accelerated and stabilized his path to power. His career was not a singular accident, but an expression of a resonance system that creates perpetrators in order to later distance itself from them and legitimize new control structures.

The existence and function of Hitler served within the resonance field as justification for far-reaching control, surveillance, and steering measures. The system used the perpetrator figure to strengthen its own steering mechanisms and reinterpret collective responsibility—the dynamic of guilt reversal was thus systemically perpetuated.

Thus, the need for control does not arise from outside, but from the system’s self-reinforcing resonance dynamics.  
→ Systemic dynamics themselves create the need for regulation—control is an emergent product of the field structure, not an individual decision.

### 10. Systemic Peacebuilding

**Path to constructive resonance spaces:**

- **System self-reflection:**  
  The system must make its own exclusion mechanisms and the production of perpetrator roles transparent. Only when the collective patterns of labeling, isolation, and guilt reversal are exposed can change begin.

- **Inclusion of all roles:**  
  A truly constructive resonance field integrates even silent, hidden, or oppositional groups. Not only the obvious actors, but also marginalized figures, the invisible, and system critics are part of the overall structure and must be actively included.

- **Breaking guilt reversal:**  
  Responsibility can no longer be projected onto individuals. Systemic peacebuilding means understanding responsibility as a collective, circular phenomenon and breaking the cycle of guilt reversal.

- **Strengthening multiperspective resonance:**  
  Different perspectives, experiences, and group affiliations are resources, not threats. Resonance spaces become constructive when diversity is valued and not labeled as a danger.

- **Making the dynamics of the control loop visible:**  
  Only when the underlying feedback and control circuits are made transparent can resonance shift from escalation to integration. Systemic capacity for peace emerges through visibility and awareness of its own mechanisms of effect.
