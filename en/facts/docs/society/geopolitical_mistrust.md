## Geopolitical Distrust and the Illusion of Diplomatic Order

---

### Abstract

The cancelled summit between Russia and Ukraine, as well as debates over international arrest warrants, illustrate: Geopolitical distrust undermines international institutions and blocks sustainable peace processes. Not only explicitly involved states, but also mediators, supranational organizations, media, shadow actors, economic networks, digital platforms, opposition camps, informal alliances, passive populations, and global resonance carriers are systemically woven into this field of distrust and blockade—even through mere self-inclusion, relation, or silent participation.

This article explains why only a conscious coupling to spiritual principles and resonance fields—such as transparency, self-reflection, mutual respect, and the integration of all structurally relevant group elements—can enable global stability. Only by making the entire resonance matrix visible does a path to sustainable order and genuine capacity for peace in the international system emerge.

---

### 1. Introduction and Case Description

In May 2025, a planned meeting between Russian President Vladimir Putin and Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky in Istanbul was cancelled at short notice. The reasons discussed by media, diplomatic circles, security services, international organizations, economic networks, civil groups, and digital platforms include security concerns and the possibility of Putin being arrested by the International Criminal Court (ICC). Opposition camps, shadow actors, supranational bodies, and passive resonance carriers are also systemically embedded in the field of decision-making and perception management—sometimes explicitly, sometimes through self-inclusion, relation, or silent participation.

In parallel, a statement by Chancellor Friedrich Merz that a visit by Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu to Germany would be possible despite an ICC arrest warrant sparked debates about the enforceability of international law. Here, state interests, media narratives, legal institutions, diplomatic resonances, economic considerations, and global power fields interlock.

These incidents raise the question of how binding and effective international norms really are when political leaders—supported or tolerated by institutions, media, shadow networks, and passive groups—can circumvent or ignore them (Ambos, 2013; Schabas, 2017). The resonance rule reveals: Every explicit decision includes an implicit field of contributors that systemically influences the effectiveness of international order.

---

### 2. Scientific Context

The enforcement of international norms depends not only on legal mechanisms but also on political and societal resonances—a field that includes explicit actors (states, institutions, courts, governments, media, NGOs) as well as implicit group elements (shadow networks, economic interest groups, digital platforms, opposition and passive groups, informal alliances, global resonance carriers). International institutions such as the ICC are effective above all when their principles are anchored in the collective consciousness, the perception and communication fields of all systemically relevant actors (Luhmann, 1997; Koskenniemi, 2002; Zürn, 2018).

Resonance field theory (Schu, 2025) adds: Sustainable order does not arise through external compulsion or the demonstration of power by individual groups, but through the synchronization of spiritual principles—transparency, self-reflection, mutual respect—and active consciousness fields of all explicit and implicit group members. True order is based on inner coherence and systemic inclusion, not authoritarian control. The concept of resonance builds on sociological approaches that understand resonance as the foundation of successful world relations and emphasize the interplay of all fields (Rosa, 2016).

---

### 3. Distrust vs. Spiritual Principles – The Dynamics of Geopolitical Fields

Distrust shapes many current conflicts. It prevents open negotiations, fosters control, manipulation, and fragmentation. Meetings are cancelled because the perceived risk is too high—trust is lacking. This dynamic is not limited to state actors: International organizations, mediators, media, shadow networks, economic interest groups, digital platforms, civil initiatives, opposition groups, and passive resonance carriers are also systemically embedded in this field of distrust and blockade—through explicit participation, relation, self-inclusion, or silent participation.

Resonance field theory emphasizes in practice:  
When spiritual principles such as transparency, self-reflection, and mutual respect are systemically activated in negotiations—not just formally, but within the consciousness field of all directly and indirectly involved—resonance and the basis for trust emerge. For example, in local peace processes, open and integrative dialogue formats (Lederach, 1997) promote sustainable understanding because they create spaces of resonance in which even implicitly involved groups and their perspectives are included. Only by consciously synchronizing all relevant fields does sustainable understanding and stability become possible.

---

### 4. Ways Out of the Blockade – Active Resonance Instead of Competition

Leaders—regardless of nation, institution, network, economic status, or media visibility—must actively break away from patterns of competition and control. Not only heads of state, but also mediators, supranational organizations, economic and civil society actors, digital platforms, shadow networks, opposition groups, and passive resonance carriers are systemically called upon to embody spiritual principles such as transparency, active listening, self-reflection, and the conscious cultivation of resonance. Only integrative communication that includes all explicit and implicit group elements creates the foundation for sustainable understanding.

Resonance field theory (Schu, 2025) shows: Whoever synchronizes consciousness fields—i.e., explicitly and implicitly couples the perception, communication, and value levels of all participants—creates sustainable, systemic stability. In practice, this is achieved through targeted trust-building, the development of shared values, and the conscious inclusion of peripheral, previously silent fields.

Peace and conflict research confirm: Trust, communication, and identity formation—understood as a collective resonance achievement of all relevant actors and fields—are central foundations of successful orders that overcome blockades and enable sustainable peace (Lederach, 1997; Mitchell, 2023).

---

### 5. Summary

Geopolitical distrust weakens international institutions, fragments structures of order, and prevents sustainable peace. The blockade affects not only heads of state, but systemically all relevant group elements: mediators, supranational organizations, economic networks, media, shadow actors, digital platforms, opposition and passive fields. Even seemingly outsiders and silent observers are, through relation, self-inclusion, or data resonance, part of the global field of uncertainty.

Only a transformation of mindset, trust, and resonance capacity—across all explicit and implicit structures—can create genuine, systemic global stability. Sustainable peace arises within the collective consciousness field, not only at summits. Only when all resonance spaces are synchronized does stable order become possible.
