## Resonance as a Path to Individual Self-Actualization  
*An Interdisciplinary Perspective on Unconscious Potentials, “Inner Messages”¹, and Societal Transformation Processes*

---

### Abstract

Unconscious impulses (“inner messages”¹) act as drivers of individual and collective development within the resonance process. From psychological, sociological, and systems theory perspectives, this article analyzes how these impulses foster self-actualization and societal change, and how resonance field theory provides a framework for interpretation.

---

### 1. The Unconscious and “Inner Messages” – Psychological Foundations

The **unconscious** stores unconscious content, biographical imprints, and archetypal patterns (Jung, 1967). Neuroscientific research shows that many decisions and creative impulses arise preconsciously (Damasio, 1994; Libet, 1985).  
Metaphorically, we speak of “inner messages”¹—indications of developmental possibilities and potentials.  
Maslow (1943, p. 382) describes self-actualization as “the need to realize one’s own potential.”

---

### 2. Resonance as a Bridge Between Inner and Outer

**Resonance** has several meanings:  
- In physics: transfer of oscillations between systems,  
- psychologically: conscious perception and integration of inner impulses,  
- sociologically: reciprocal relationship between subject and environment, in which both change (Rosa, 2016, p. 298).

Experiences of resonance—such as in creative flow (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) or through social connectedness—have been shown to increase creativity, innovative power, and well-being. Studies indicate that flow experiences can increase creativity by up to 30% (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990). Positive psychology and empirical studies further confirm that resonance and connectedness correlate with higher life satisfaction (Seligman, 2011).

---

### 3. Control, Compulsion, and Resonance Rupture

External control and social pressure to conform impair intrinsic motivation and resonance capacity (Deci & Ryan, 2000). Empirical findings show: Where autonomy, competence, and connectedness are lacking, a resonance rupture threatens—with increased risk of burnout, depression, or cynicism (Rosa, 2016; Seligman, 2011).

---

### 4. Bridging to Resonance Field Theory

Resonance field theory sees individuals as nodes in a dynamic resonance network, enabling self-actualization as a contribution to collective evolution (Schu, 2025).

---

### 5. From Observer to Resonator

Those who move from passive observer to active resonator take their inner voice seriously, tolerate contradictions, and enter into creative exchange with the social resonance field. Self-actualization here is a contribution to collective consciousness development, not narcissistic self-involvement.

---

### Examples & Empirical Evidence

- Positive psychology: Flow experiences increase creativity by up to 30% (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990, https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.45.4.359).
- Systemic therapy: Resonance between client and therapist demonstrably improves therapy outcomes (Simon & Rech-Simon, 2013).
- Occupational psychology: Autonomy and resonance at the workplace significantly increase job satisfaction (Deci & Ryan, 2000).

---

### Glossary

- **Inner Message:** Metaphor for unconscious impulses and potentials that stimulate self-development.
- **Resonance:**  
  – Physical: transfer of oscillations between systems,  
  – psychological: conscious perception and integration of inner impulses,  
  – sociological: reciprocal, transformative relationship between subject and environment.
- **Self-Actualization:** Striving to realize one’s own potential (Maslow).
- **Systems Theory:** Science of describing complex interactions in open systems (Luhmann, 1984).
