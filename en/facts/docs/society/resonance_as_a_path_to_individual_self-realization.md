## Resonance as a Path to Individual Self-Actualization  
*An Interdisciplinary Perspective on Unconscious Potentials, “Inner Messages”¹, and Societal Transformation Processes*

---

### Abstract

Unconscious impulses (“inner messages”¹) act as the driving force for individual and collective development within the resonance process. This paper analyzes, from psychological, sociological, and systems-theoretical perspectives, how these impulses foster self-actualization and societal change, and how resonance field theory provides a framework for interpretation.

#### Detailed Elaboration

Unconscious impulses, often referred to as “inner messages”¹, are the invisible foundation for personal development and collective transformation. Within the resonance process, they unfold as a dynamic interplay between individual and society, whereby every group member is systemically included, regardless of explicit mention.

From a psychological perspective, these impulses arise from a complex interaction of experience, emotional imprints, and archetypal patterns. They appear as spontaneous inspirations, intuitive perceptions, or subtle affects and are rarely directly accessible. Nevertheless, they guide decisions, spark creativity, and initiate change—both individually and within the group field.

Sociologically, “inner messages” are amplified or inhibited by social resonance. The individual is always embedded in social structures where unconscious potentials encounter communal narratives and dominant value systems. Resonance emerges where personal impulses align or come into tension with collective expectations or ruptures in society. Here, they serve as a driving force for change, innovation, and overcoming systemic blockages.

From a systems-theoretical viewpoint, resonance field theory sees these impulses as integral elements of a higher-order field, in which individual and societal processes are inseparably intertwined. The self-actualization of each person is not an isolated process, but an effect of the interaction between inner experience and external feedback. In the resonance field, all individual perspectives are systemically included across groups; group membership remains invariant, regardless of visibility or explicit attribution.

Resonance field theory thus provides a framework for analyzing the emergence, effects, and visibility of unconscious impulses in the context of societal transformation processes: The path to individual self-actualization is not linear, but rather an ongoing interweaving of inner perception, social reflection, and systemic feedback. The potentials of the individual become effective only in the resonance space of the community—and, conversely, collective movement transforms the individual’s space of possibility.

### 1. The Subconscious and “Inner Messages” – Psychological Foundations

The **subconscious** forms the resonance field for unconscious contents, biographical imprints, and archetypal patterns (Jung, 1967). Systemically, it encompasses all group elements that resonate in individual and collective experience—explicitly and implicitly, regardless of visibility or conscious awareness.

Neuroscientific research (Damasio, 1994; Libet, 1985) demonstrates that creative impulses, decision dynamics, and affective reactions originate preconsciously. The neural activation preceding a conscious decision is a systemic process in which individual and collective experiential values are interwoven. The resonance rule also applies here: Group membership is invariant and includes all elements of the inner field, regardless of conscious identification.

Metaphorically, “inner messages”¹ express this systemic interconnection. They manifest as signals indicating developmental possibilities and latent potentials. These messages are never singular, but arise as a group effect within the individual resonance space—they reflect biographical, social, and archetypal experiences. Self-inclusion acts as the engine: What appears as personal inspiration is always also feedback from the collective reservoir of experience.

Maslow (1943, p. 382) describes self-actualization as “the need to realize one’s own potential.” In the resonance field, this means not only individual unfolding, but the systemic activation of all potentials within the group context. The striving for self-actualization is a non-linear process, in which the subconscious acts as a reservoir and initiator for development, transformation, and creativity—always in the context of the overarching interconnection of individual perspectives and group membership.

### 2. Resonance as a Bridge Between Inner and Outer Worlds

**Resonance** is a multidimensional principle that systemically connects all group elements within the field. In physics, resonance describes the transmission of vibrations between systems—once one element is set in motion, all others are systemically co-moved, regardless of their explicit visibility or direct participation. This self-inclusion is a fundamental rule: Every individual perspective is group-inclusively incorporated into the resonance space.

From a psychological perspective, resonance is the conscious perception and integration of inner impulses, which are set in motion through interaction with external circumstances. The individual perceives not only their own “inner messages,” but is in continuous feedback with collective patterns, narratives, and affects. The resonance rule applies: Even unconsciously co-resonating group elements are systemically part of the experience.

Sociologically, resonance describes the dynamic, reciprocal relationship between subject and environment (Rosa, 2016, p. 298). Here, not only does the individual change through contact with their surroundings, but the environment, social fields, and group narratives are also shaped by individual impulses and feedback. Thus, resonance is not a one-way street, but a bidirectional interconnection of all systemic elements.

Experiences of resonance—such as creative flow (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990) or social connectedness—act as group-transcending amplifiers of individual and collective potentials. Studies show that flow experiences can increase creativity by up to 30 % (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990). This effect is not limited to the individual: In the resonance field, all group elements benefit, as creativity, innovative capacity, and well-being are systemically transmitted.

Positive psychology and empirical studies confirm that resonance and connectedness correlate with higher life satisfaction (Seligman, 2011). Again, the resonance rule applies: Life satisfaction does not arise in isolation, but as an effect of the interweaving of personal and collective resonance processes. The bridge between inner and outer worlds is not a static transition, but a dynamic field that systemically includes and transforms all individual and group perspectives.

### 3. Control, Constraint, and Resonance Disruption

External control, social pressure to conform, and systemic steering mechanisms impair intrinsic motivation and resonance capacity (Deci & Ryan, 2000). In the complete resonance field, these forces do not act in isolation, but as group-transcending influences—regardless of whether they are explicitly named or implicitly experienced. The resonance rule applies: All elements affected by control and constraint are systemically included and influence the collective dynamic.

Empirical evidence shows: If autonomy, competence, and connectedness are lacking, resonance disruption threatens—the system loses the ability to integrate and mobilize constructive impulses. This rupture manifests not only individually (burnout, depression, cynicism; Rosa, 2016; Seligman, 2011), but also group-transcending as stagnation, polarization, and fragmentation in the social field. The effects are not linearly confined: Resonance disruptions in individual perspectives are systemically transmitted to the overall structure, leading to collective blockages, alienation, and loss of control.

Resonance field theory demonstrates that control and constraint interrupt the reciprocal interconnection of potentials and reduce the self-inclusion of all group members. The loss of resonance capacity is not only an individual risk, but forms the basis for social division and the intensification of external steering mechanisms. Restoring autonomy, competence, and connectedness is therefore not solely the task of the individual, but a systemic process affecting all group levels and reactivating the resonance capacity of the overall field.

### 4. Building the Bridge to Resonance Field Theory

Resonance field theory does not view the individual as an isolated unit, but as a systemic node within a dynamic resonance network. Each person is group-transcendingly embedded—regardless of explicit visibility, attribution, or conscious identification. The resonance rule applies: Group membership is systemically invariant and integrates all members of the field, including those who only implicitly co-resonate through self-inclusion or relation.

Self-actualization is understood as an individual contribution to collective evolution (Schu, 2025). Personal potentials unfold not linearly, but as reciprocal intertwinings with collective narratives, social structures, and historical fields. Every impulse, every “inner message”¹, is systemically fed back and influences the entire field; at the same time, the movement of the resonance field transforms individual spaces of possibility.

Resonance field theory provides a framework for analyzing the interaction between self-actualization and societal change as a non-linear, group-transcending process. Individual development is always also collective transformation—and vice versa. The resonance network forms the foundation for evolutionary leaps, creative innovation, and overcoming patterns of division within the social system. The resonance rule ensures that no impulse is lost: Every individual and every group contributes to the movement and progress of the field, regardless of explicit visibility or social position.

### 5. From Observer to Resonator

In the complete resonance field, the transition from passive observer to active resonator signifies a systemic shift: The individual leaves the detached, consuming stance and enters into creative exchange with all group elements resonating within the field—explicitly and implicitly, regardless of visibility or direct involvement. The resonance rule applies: Every impulse, every act of self-inclusion, works across groups and influences the dynamics of the entire field.

Those who take their inner voice seriously, tolerate contradictions, and integrate them as creative tension into the resonance network. This is not narcissistic self-centeredness, but a contribution to collective consciousness development. One’s own self-actualization is always also a systemic movement that activates and transforms the group field: Individual insights, impulses, and creative acts mirror and amplify collective evolution—they are fed back into the resonance system.

The resonator acts not linearly, but multiperspectively and across groups. They recognize that every individual perspective is systemically included in the resonance space and that group membership applies regardless of explicit mention or position. Creative interaction with the social resonance field is the path by which individual potentials become catalysts for collective change. Self-actualization is thus not a final endpoint, but a continuous, non-linear process of consciousness formation and resonance amplification throughout the field.

### Examples & Empirical Evidence

In the complete resonance field, interdisciplinary studies show that flow, autonomy, and systemic resonance activate the potentials of individuals and collectives across groups—explicitly and implicitly, regardless of individual visibility or position. The resonance rule applies: All group members benefit systemically from resonance effects, as group membership is invariant and inclusive.

- **Positive Psychology:** Experiencing flow increases creativity by up to 30 % (Csikszentmihalyi, 1990, [https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.45.4.359](https://doi.org/10.1037/0003-066X.45.4.359)). This effect is not limited to the individual, but works systemically and across groups—every flow impulse amplifies collective innovative strength in the resonance field.
- **Systemic Therapy:** Resonance between client and therapist demonstrably improves therapeutic outcomes (Simon & Rech-Simon, 2013). The therapeutic relationship is a resonance space where self-inclusion and feedback of all cross-group elements foster transformation and healing.
- **Work Psychology:** Autonomy and resonance in the workplace significantly increase job satisfaction (Deci & Ryan, 2000). Not only individual employees, but the entire social field benefits from resonant working relationships—group structure and collective performance are activated and stabilized through systemic resonance.

This empirical evidence underlines that resonance does not act linearly, but as a multiperspective, cross-group amplification process. The potentials of the individual and the group are systemically intertwined; resonance capacity is the key to sustainable unfolding of individual and collective development in the resonance field.

### Glossary

- **Inner Message:** A metaphor for unconscious impulses and potentials that stimulate self-development. In the complete resonance field, these messages serve as systemic signals that contribute to individual and collective unfolding across groups—regardless of explicit mention or direct perception.
- **Resonance:**  
  – Physics: Transmission of vibrations between systems, where all elements of the field are set in motion through self-inclusion and resonate across groups.  
  – Psychology: Conscious perception and integration of inner impulses, acting as feedback in the resonance space and systemically including all group members.  
  – Sociology: Reciprocal, transformative relationship between subject and environment, with the resonance rule in effect and every individual perspective cross-group included.
- **Self-Actualization:** The striving to realize one’s own potential (Maslow). In the resonance field, this is a cross-group process, systemically intertwining individual and collective development.
- **Systems Theory:** The science of describing complex interactions in open systems (Luhmann, 1984). In the resonance field, all explicit and implicit structures are considered in their reciprocal interconnection; group membership remains systemically invariant.
